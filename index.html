Cybersecurity Education Research Summary

Effectiveness of CTF Gamification Platform vs. Traditional Learning Methods

Research Overview

This study evaluated the effectiveness of a Capture The Flag (CTF) gamification platform for teaching cybersecurity competencies compared to traditional lecture-based methods. The research employed an experimental design with control and experimental groups, using various statistical analyses to evaluate learning outcomes.

The experimental group (using CTF platform) demonstrated greater overall improvement in cybersecurity competencies compared to the control group (traditional lectures), particularly in practical skill development.

Participant Background

  • Equal distribution of prior CTF experience (50% had previous experience)
  • Majority of experienced participants (60.6%) identified as beginners
  • Web Exploitation (65.6%) and Cryptography (59.4%) were the most familiar areas
  • High interest in learning cybersecurity skills (mean rating: 3.88/5)

Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test

Both control and experimental groups showed significant improvements in cybersecurity knowledge and skills between pre-test and post-test:

  • Control group overall improvement: p = 0.002
  • Experimental group overall improvement: p < 0.001

Mann-Whitney U Test

Despite improvements in both groups, the differences between experimental and control groups were not statistically significant (p > 0.05).

Participant Feedback

Experimental Group (CTF Platform)

  • 94.1% reported increased interest in cybersecurity
  • 94.1% rated the gamified approach as highly or extremely engaging
  • 88.2% recommended integrating CTF into traditional lectures
  • Web Exploitation and Forensics were rated as most enjoyable (29.4% each)
  • Reverse Engineering was identified as most challenging (88.2%)

Control Group (Traditional Learning)

  • 83.3% rated the traditional method as effective or very effective
  • 94.4% recommended incorporating gamified methods
  • OSINT showed highest perceived improvement (72.2%)
  • Reverse Engineering showed lowest perceived improvement (38.9%)

Conclusions

The research demonstrates that while traditional lecture-based approaches produce meaningful learning outcomes, integrating hands-on gamified activities through CTF platforms yields superior results, particularly for practical skill development.

Both teaching methods struggled with complex topics like Reverse Engineering, suggesting a need for specialized instructional strategies in this area.

The overwhelmingly positive feedback from both groups regarding gamification suggests strong potential for integrating CTF-style activities into cybersecurity education curriculum.
